Case Study

Rewinder Accuracy Root-Cause Investigation

Identified the source of systematic length error across production rewinders by validating PLC distance logic, encoder behavior, and physical machine geometry before supplier-side correction.

This project was one of my first major real factory troubleshooting investigations. I did not own the OEM corrective implementation, but I led the investigation that made corrective action possible.

Why It Mattered

In our production flow, rewinders were used before and after multiple process steps for measuring tape length, cutting material, and isolating bad-quality areas. Because raw Hastelloy came in kilograms rather than accurately measured length, rewinder accuracy directly affected both cost-per-meter and quality handling decisions.

The problem was first noticed because tape length information after different process stages did not match, including discrepancies observed around EP, IBAD, and copper-plating-related work.

Challenge

Production rewinders were reporting inconsistent tape lengths. The main challenge was that the error could have come from several places: PLC math, encoder counting, motion hardware behavior, or the mechanical geometry of the rewinder itself.

The investigation had to be done carefully because a wrong conclusion would waste engineering time and potentially push the factory toward the wrong fix.

Engineering Approach

I approached the problem by ruling out possible causes step by step instead of assuming the issue was purely mechanical or purely software.

I first verified the controller-side logic and math used for travelled distance and line speed. Then I checked technical hardware details such as maximum RPM and response-frequency assumptions. After that, I wrote a computer-vision script to independently count reel revolutions and compare them against PLC-side counting. I also checked encoder pulse consistency directly. Only after those paths remained consistent did I move to physical caliper measurements of the top wheel.

What I Found

The root cause was not PLC miscalculation and not encoder inconsistency. The real issue was effective top-wheel diameter variation, especially the difference between the diameter at the middle contact region where the tape actually travelled and the perpendicular/side measurement.

That meant a rewinder could look dimensionally acceptable from one measurement method while still producing accumulated length error in real operating conditions.

Up to +6.29 m Error observed over 2500 m in another comparison set

Result

I produced the technical evidence package and presentation that established the root cause and supported supplier-side corrective action. The machine builder later applied a calibration-factor-based correction using a 1000 m reference tape.

My contribution was the investigation, proof structure, and root-cause isolation that enabled the responsible OEM to act with confidence.

My Role vs OEM Responsibility

I did not implement the final OEM correction myself. Supplier ownership remained with Sanki. My role was to identify the true failure mode, validate it with evidence, and communicate the findings clearly enough for corrective action to be taken by the responsible machine builder.

What This Project Demonstrates

This project shows how I approach production problems as a systems engineer: checking control logic, validating machine behavior independently, and connecting physical hardware reality back to automation assumptions. It also reflects an important engineering habit I want to carry forward: separate root-cause identification from ownership of final corrective action.
